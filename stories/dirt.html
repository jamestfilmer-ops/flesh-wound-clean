Story

DIRT

Nov 16, 2025  ·  4 min read

The best. That's what he wanted — nothing subpar, nothing lesser; only prestige, simply masterclass. He wanted her to be the best, nothing short of it, and she had worked every day of her life to be exactly that.

She ran the back fields before school. She practiced her cuts in the hallway, her mother calling from the kitchen to stop wearing tracks in the carpet. She watched film on her laptop, the glow of it the last light in the house.

By fourteen she was the fastest girl in the county. By sixteen, recruiters were sitting in the bleachers with clipboards and quiet eyes, watching her like they were calculating something.

"Despite the summer heat and dirt clinging to her sweaty skin, she felt chills, goosebumps rising on her arms and legs."

The day of the regional finals it was ninety-two degrees. The infield was dry and cracked. She stood at the starting blocks and looked down at her shoes, at the red clay dust that had worked itself into every crease, and she thought about her father watching from the far end of the stands, hands folded in his lap the way he always held them when he was trying not to show that he cared.

The gun cracked.

She didn't hear the crowd. She never did. There was just the rhythm of her own breathing, the burn starting low in her thighs, the finish line coming toward her like something inevitable, like something she had already done a thousand times in the dark of her room with the laptop glow on her face.

She won by two strides.

In the moment after — that small, suspended moment before anything becomes real — she bent forward, hands on her knees, and felt the heat of the track radiating back up at her. The dirt on her legs. The dust in her lungs.

Her father reached her before anyone else. He didn't say anything. He just put his hand on the back of her head the way he used to when she was small.

· · ·

She told this story once, years later, to a room full of girls who were fourteen and fifteen and wanted to know what it felt like to be the best.

"It felt like dirt," she said. "Like dirt and summer heat and your dad's hand on your head."

They looked at her like they expected something else.

She didn't have anything else to give them.
