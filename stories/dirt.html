DIRT

The best. That's what he wanted. The best, nothing subpar, nothing lesser; only prestige, simply masterclass. It was hard to please Dr. Carvill.

August 9th, 1958

The butterflies in Paisley's stomach were not from nervous excitement. No, the last time she felt those was when Brady Jenkins asked her to the Junior Prom. These were bees, yellowjackets, even, swarming rampantly. Uneasiness met with subtle, but nonetheless sharp, pangs of terror as she drove atop the dusty soil on Oil Springs Road. Despite the summer heat and dirt clinging to her sweaty skin, she felt chills, goosebumps rising on her arms and legs.

Perhaps this is normal, she thought. Heading into the middle of the Wyoming desert with a guy you met last week, camping with a professor rumored to have a screw loose and a drinking problem — under these circumstances, anyone without fear would be worth studying. Keep your eye on the prize, Pais.

Cameron awoke, opening his eyes and positioning himself slowly into the center of the seat. Paisley's Nash Metropolitan was not the most comfortable. He meant to ask her about the car — where she bought it, how long she had owned it, mainly — but given the torn seats, the dents and cracked windows, he wondered how she would take it. He did not know her well, only she was not well-off. Of course, though, neither was he. They both were alone in this world, after all.

The class of students accepted in Carvill's infamous "Summer Expedition" was small. A few of the other attendees offered him a seat, but they were not exactly the crowd who would make a twelve and a half hour drive bearable. Paisley, for her standoffish demeanor and lack of beautification — her angular clothing, the lack of powder on her face — attracted him. When he learned she would be driving the powder blue Nash alone, he asked if she would want company. With a hint of reluctance, she agreed.

Paisley heard Cameron shift in his seat and immediately reached for the radio. If there was any chance to avoid conversation, she was ready to take it. After a few clicks turning for a channel, it became clear there was only static in the air.

"So, uh, what is your favorite movie?" Cameron fired a flare into the mute conversation. To Paisley, it felt like a cannon ball hitting still water.

"Don't watch 'em much." Did she sound like a battleaxe? His voice, for a moment, had quieted the yellowjackets.

"Although," she started, "Rear Window was good."

Cameron agreed.

For the next few hours, they made great conversation.

· · ·

Two wooden signs marked the roadways where students were to deviate off the dirt road onto a pathway that could best be described as the spirit of a road. Soon, they spotted Dr. Carvill's pickup truck and a red Ford Thunderbird, its white walls now beige, stained by dirt.

Paisley got out of the car and was immediately overcome with a sense of dizziness and nausea — the heat devoured her whole.

Taking her suitcase from the trunk of the car, the lack of land fixtures or civilization was only emphasized by the dirt and gravel under her boots. Never had she known emptiness to be this imposing.

Closing the lid of the trunk felt like putting your hand on a burning stove; she wondered if the tires would melt at this rate. Her mouth felt metallic, and upon glancing at the tents and, a little further off, the ribbons and stakes marking the current dig, the stinging in her gut continued. It was brief, but for a second, she wanted to reach for Cameron's hand.

· · ·

All the studying had led to this. Who knew a desert so engrained with death could lead to new life? The careers, the journals, the accolades and fortune — Carvill's mind held the key for the next generation of paleontologists. His discoveries were world famous, and his techniques for locating viable fossil sites built the University of Oklahoma's paleontology department to be the gold standard. Only the gifted were admitted to the graduate program, and even fewer received the invitation to Carvill's "summer home."

Now he was here. Cameron Bodega, standing a few feet above his future. In four of the last seven years, National Geographic had featured Carvill and his students in lengthy exposés — those photographed now sitting in tenured positions at natural history museums across the country, even the Smithsonian.

In the distance, amidst the clear blue sky, he saw the moon, transparent but gaining hold as dusk approached hour by hour. Tonight, he would see more beauty above than most do in a lifetime; yet he would only be concerned with that which lied trapped in the cold, dark earth.

August 10th, 1958

The students stood outside their tents in the morning haze, the sun just peaking over the horizon. Carvill's white Ford left a trail of amber dust in its wake as he approached from his trailer, purportedly located a few miles closer to the nearest watering hole.

It took only a few minutes to understand the whispers, the allegations. Why some students, despite their later success, regretted their participation in a Carvill dig.

Carvill drank — it seemed Carvill's hand was glued to his flask. Carvill smoked — his cracked, leathery lips shaped to hold a Camel in the corner. Carvill was only Mr. Hyde, even in the daytime, leaving Dr. Jekyll in Oklahoma. The worst, however, was Carvill's voice; never satisfied, spoken from a tongue that treated decency as foreign.

The crowds in Paisley's life story were far from loving and cordial. She was not fazed by his coarse language, the insults that could make jailbirds weep. Even a grabbing of the wrist or a small shove could be ignored, but not everyone was as resilient.

He nearly fell out of the Ford; his legs only vaguely appeared to be cooperating with the rest of his body. His eyes locked on Leslie, of the girls from the Thunderbird's group.

"Did you know this was a dig site?" Carvill screeched. "Why are you dressed like a streetwalker?" Unabated, unashamed, the end of his cigarette fiery red.

"S-s-s-Sorry, Dr. Carvill," Leslie whimpered. The rest looked on, like baby pigs gazing at the farmer pick up the runt and lead it from the barn. Leslie tried so desperately to break eye contact, Carvill's eyes dilated and stampeded over her.

"I believed this attire would be a-a-acceptable based on the syllabus and former students." Tears welled in her eyes.

"Oh, did you? You heeeeaar that everyone!?" His voice aimed for the heavens, his vocal cords oiled with Southern Comfort, the bottle in his cupholder. "Loose Leslie thinks she's here for a date!" The corners of his mouth and eyes cracked into a smile, quick and unnerving, like shattered glass.

Taking one longing look at her tucked in button-up blouse and jean shorts down just above the knee, Carvill forgot all about his dress code, and staggered forth towards the distant site.

"Now, let me show you your assignments. Your tickets to glory!" There was no mistaking he believed this to be a gift — an olive branch of sorts, for the abuse they may endure.

· · ·

In the wall of the trench, angular bones jettisoned out into the sun. At about eight feet long and who knew how tall, Paisley was stunned by the symmetry and condition the skeleton exhibited. It was like nothing they had ever seen.

"Proeliator Lacertus — the Warrior Lizard." It was magnificent. Entombed in rusty clay and dirt lay a specimen millions of years old. Small quivers ran down the exposed spine.

Even Leslie smiled, her wounds seemingly repaired only minutes after the public lashing. No hard feelings, here was her future. Lips sealed.

Within Paisley, the sight of Lacertus turned the harsh sting of yellowjackets to the flutter of butterfly wings.

August 20th, 1958

After a few days, a rhythm began to form. Awaking at the first sign of light, the students retreated from their individual tents, ate breakfast, filled up their canteens from jugs Carvill dropped off the night before, and trekked towards the site. Carvill would join momentarily, the hum of the Ford unmistakable. He would lead them to their assignments for the day. On a rotation, some students worked on Lacertus, while others uncovered smaller creatures spread out amid the basin, or cleaned tools. Now, ten days in, Carvill assumed a more laissez-faire attitude.

In two of the last five days, he was rarely present, driving off after checking in with the students and their progress, only arriving at lunch and dinner to deliver water and food rations. If he felt led, or too drunk at the moment to drive, he walked around, viewing their progress.

Today, Carvill assigned the two of them to a section he believed to be a femur of Lacertus. He also mentioned to be on the lookout for any more teeth nearby, like those Leslie had found the day prior.

Paisley, her burgundy canvas bag in hand, dropped down into the shallow canal next to Cameron. He wore a forest green tee shirt and a white Cardinals ball cap. While at first glance wearing a white hat in these conditions seemed absurd, Paisley got the sense Cameron wanted to remember this dig, his first. In fifty years, he wanted the rust-stained fibers to tell the story to his grandchildren.

Around eleven, Carvill appeared, elbows on his hips, addressing Cameron.

"Hey, kid, whatcha' think? You and your little squeeze ain't making a mess of my life's work, are ya?"

With no hesitation, Cameron looked down at the exposed bone. "Well, Paisley is continuing to brush this side, and I am going to start chiseling at the base to perhaps begin extrac—" a rock barreled down inches from Cameron's head.

"A chisel? Are you mad? The greater trochanter is halfway out the damn dirt, and you want to carve it like you're making the statue of David?" Every fiber of Carvill's being seemed at the edge of snapping. His ligaments looked detached at the sockets, he was a loose cannon, a reptile standing before them with slit pupils, shaking in rage.

In a mocking tone, he began, "Ooh my I see a pretty lady I should chop up all Dr. Carvill's good work. WELL, DO YOU WANT A CHISEL? YEAH?" He jumped into the trench towards the pair, grabbed the chisel from Cameron's belt, and tossed it towards the tents. "NO ONE carves up my bones!" He spat, and as if his body could not bear standing any longer, he sat on the edge of the pit for the remainder of the afternoon, watching them.

Not a word was said despite his unwavering stare. Smoking his cigarettes to the tip of his fingers, he flung them with disregard into the pit to later be dug up by students. They dug with soft brushes the entire afternoon and until the sun no longer shined on the horizon.

He saw them to the tents.

· · ·

From his truck, meals were rationed out — old MREs. Paisley doubted they were fresh. The smell was hideous. Just as every night before, Dr. Carvill drove off to the trailer no one had seen after handing out dinner. The students, with no sense for conversation, went to their tents, most of them only pondering the paper in which their names would appear in short order.

Cameron and Paisley did not speak of the incident until, later that evening, Cameron heard shuffling outside.

"Psst."

Cameron opened his tent. The moon adorned the landscape in shades of blue and steel. Above, millions of stars glistened — a sequin tapestry — and yet, his focus lay on Paisley, crouched before him.

"I have an idea."

August 22nd, 1958

As Carvill handed out rations under the mess tent, Paisley and Cameron were not in attendance. They were "pursuing extracurriculars" as Carvill put it. It was not the first time students of his had made do in the lonely summer expanse. He shared more obscenities to the students as he unloaded the food.

Walking back to his truck, flask and cigarette in hand, he mumbled obscenities.

The churning amber cloud of dust laid at their feet. Cameron and Paisley struggled not to laugh, though they freely smiled under the tarp.

The wooden horse, with its hidden load of warriors.

· · ·

When the truck came to a stop, the two stow-aways were surprised to hear a woman's voice, demanding yet sweet — Carvill had a companion. By lifting an inch of the tarp on the side of the truck, Cameron could see there was not one but two trailers. One was despondent, dark, unkempt, and the other, an Airstream holding the voice of a woman like a shell holds the noise of the sea, was alight and warm.

They waited cautiously before moving an inch. Who was she? Carvill never mentioned a woman, and, given his demeanor, it was assumed by all he walked this earth as lonely as the bones he sought. Paisley had presumed Carvill's loneliness allowed him to find the destitute; they called out for each other.

Nevertheless, there was her voice. A door opened and a door shut. The silhouette of a man and a woman became clear through the thinly-draped windows.

Even more surprising, they sounded somewhat happy.

August 23rd, 1958

An hour passed, then two, then a few more. Not until the sky went black and the night felt still did they consider executing the next step of their plan. Cameron's watch stated it was about one in the morning. They slid from the tarp and crept towards the unoccupied trailer.

The plan was crude and adolescent in nature. They wanted solely to send a message, an anonymous yet clear deed of displeasure. Yet, the plan had changed. Now they were given access to a new dimension, a portal to Carvill's Fortress of Solitude.

Curiosity pulled them, it forced them; the unknown became a magnet to their hearts, and they needed to pay its fee, needed to venture in. After all, wonder can be childlike and magnificent, ill-fated and malicious; the discovery of new cures and the study of the dead — both driven by an unsatiated will to know, to see.

They both wondered what lay within the dark trailer.

Cameron and Paisley approached the rotten structure and tried the door.

· · ·

The floor was more earth than wood, a layer of dirt matted under their feet. Luckily, there was no need for a light as there were no drapes on the windows, and the moon shined through just fine.

Shovels and trowels lined the wall opposite the door. There was no furniture other than a folding lawn chair in the corner, surrounded by empty bottles. Newspaper clippings and sepia tone photographs featuring Carvill's most famous discoveries were taped all around. On each wall stood a work bench with tools. The right held chisels, brushes, and a few pairs of latex gloves; the left displayed saws, wood files — a bucket of paint — and a white skull surrounded by vertebrae, also sand-dollar white.

A horse's head, parts of its spine.

In a nearby cup, she noticed its teeth had been removed and filed into points. These are the ones Leslie found, Paisley thought. He brought them back with him; but she knew now there was more to the story. She knew, feeling them again, the point against her fingertip, amidst the others — beside the white, dead skull — these may be what Leslie found, but they had not been in the ground for millennia.

She made eye contact with Cameron. He was holding the bucket of paint, now, examining it.

POTASSIUM BICHROMATE

Cameron now understood. It was dye.

The man was a fraud. A charlatan. A liar and a cheat. He understood now — Carvill's woman. While he taught, she buried. While the students dug, they created. The discoveries and the accolades, all a sham. Carvill, a modern Victor Frankenstein, pursuing dead creatures to preserve his life. The students acted as launderers, verifying the findings. Every few years they might move the trailers a few hours away so as not to arise suspicion, but who would know? His reputation, the digs of the past, of his early days before he was the Dr. Carvill — no one would second guess at risk of their reputation, let alone a student looking to the expert for help.

Cameron was enraged, Paisley bewildered. They were so enthralled and mystified by what they saw; they did not hear Carvill return or the click of the blinding bulb.

They would not see the sun again.

June 4th, 2025

Hallow County Sheriff Department,

At this time, we believe we have identified the bodies discovered in Hallow County, Wyoming two weeks ago. We believe these to be the remains of Paisley Winter and Cameron Bodega — two University of Oklahoma students, who went missing before the Fall Semester in 1958. The remains of what is believed to be Paisley's 1953 Nash Metropolitan were also found.

Based on preliminary findings, it seems Dr. Jacob Carvill and his wife, Regina Carvill, are the most likely suspects at this time.

Paisley and Cameron's disappearance was believed to be the result of adolescent impulsivity. At the time, Carvill was questioned, but his story was independently corroborated by students on the expedition, who believed it highly likely the pair, growing ever friendlier, drove off in the night, dropping out of school, each other's only real community.

We now believe Carvill, and his wife, murdered Paisley and Cameron, took Paisley's keys from her person and drove her '53 Nash to the trailers. It was disassembled over the span of a few months and buried at various locations in the surrounding area. Paisley and Cameron's belongings were then collected the following day; students recalled him stating frustration the pair had not "cleaned up after themselves."

Dr. Carvill was stripped of tenure following independent studies of his work in 1974, which left much room for doubt into the veracity of his findings.

Him and his wife both passed away in 1987.

The University of Oklahoma has been fully cooperative in this investigation, and we are considering this case closed currently.
