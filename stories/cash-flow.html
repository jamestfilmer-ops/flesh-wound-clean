Prologue — Part I

La Vie En Rose plays as it corrupts the silence. The ceiling fan wisps around. The smell of paint sticks to the nostrils. The only ventilation system is the window, cracked only about an inch — the best Ferris can do in his small studio apartment. The vapor from the oil awakens the brain. The window is always crystal clear, for it is how Ferris sees the world. Too scared to go outside, the window serves as his looking glass into 1950s Chicago. From here, he can see the streetlights stretch for miles, the locomotives whizzing by, and the constant spectacle of pedestrians.

Ferris had not ceased painting since June 2nd, 1950 — the day of his parents' funerals. His mother and father, an incredibly talented actress and an extremely successful financial investor, respectively, were venturing to dinner when they were killed by a streetcar after it collided with a fuel truck at the corner of 63rd and State Street. Boom. They were just two of thirty-four civilians who were killed. It remains one of the deadliest automobile accidents in Chicago to date. A few weeks later, the wills were read to the beneficiaries — Ferris; his two older sisters, Marilyn, and Cecelia; and Marvin, the eldest. Being that they were so wealthy, the dividends of the estate among the four siblings was rather hefty — a crisp $350,000, which in 1950, was just about the equivalent to $3.7 million.

The lawyer left, and the siblings sat in their then-luxurious high rise until the sun bathed the room in sunset orange, and eventually vanished altogether. Silence; no one spoke a word. And, although they stayed motionless with one another in what seemed like a unified grieving, their heads were all in different places.

Marvin: I'm headed to Vegas, gonna' find a wife, get hitched, and never have to worry again. With that kind of money, I can't lose! I could get into the movie picture business. No, I got it: I'll build my own hotel! "The Suites of Love". I like it!

Marilyn: I have always wanted to set up my own floral shop. I can finally get out of this dirty city. New York, here I come! The Marylin Boutique. I can live with mom's actress friend and get my feet on the ground. $350,000 could get me a store with a little waterfall in the center — something cliché and Roman. Cupid holding roses spitting water out of his mouth. I love stupid decorations like that. The floor won't be this ugly carpet that they typically have. Oh, I got it, the back room can be a salon as well. A koi pond? I can't wait! Thank you, mom and dad!

Cecelia: Imagine when I tell Roonie! Oh, he'll be so excited! "Hey, sweetie! I got you The Cubs for Christmas! You turn those boys around!" I could buy a boat, a Thunderbird! Oh, LORD! THANK YA! We could have kids, a family, a library, a study. Oh, maybe he'll want a boat, or a bright crimson Mercedes.

Ferris: I miss mom and dad.

Prologue — Part II

Marvin bought a plane ticket to Las Vegas. He did not write, he did not call, he changed his name. Seally was his new surname, Jim his first. When he arrived in McCarran Airport, there was a shiny Thunderbird waiting to take him to his hotel. The lights lit up like stars in the night sky. He had his entire new fortune in his carry-on. Hopping in the back of the car, he immediately dropped his bag on the torn leather seat beside him. Seally was now locked in. He could not look away from Vegas. His eyes turned emerald green.

They arrived at the hotel. Of course, the entrance led right into a casino. The bellboy offered to take his luggage, but there was no way in hell Seally was letting go of $350,000.

"I'll be okay. Thank you. Here, you have a good night." Seally said as he reached into his pocket, grabbed a $1.50 and a candy wrapper and handed it to the nineteen-year-old, who would later become the owner of the hotel. His kids would never have to work again.

Seally took his luggage and dragged it behind him. He was a gentleman who dressed nice and wore a gold watch. His father had given him the watch as a birthday gift a few years ago when he was eighteen. On the back of the watch it said, Wisdom is the key to happiness and wealth. In all honesty, that watch could have said "Ferrets drink cocaine." Seally never would have known the difference. He liked how the watch shimmered in the lights of the casino.

Making his way over to the blackjack table, Seally sat on what seemed like a cloud. The table seat glistened green with metallic flakes that drove the eyes wild. Hunching over, he unzipped his bag and pulled out a stack of one-hundred-dollar bills. Oh, he was so excited to put that on the table. The dealer slid the empire of red chips into Seally's hands.

"Hit me." He cried out. He was practically jumping. An Ace appeared from the sky.

"HIT ME!" A king. He felt like a king.

"Hit me!"

Within the hour, Seally had made up his money. He was up about twenty-five grand from when he started. When he decided the game was too easy, he made his way to the tables to play poker.

As a child, Marvin had played a lot of poker. They would meet in the bathroom during lunch period at his private school. Truth be told, he never won, but this was Vegas and he had just made out with enough money to buy himself a gold Skylark, so confidence levels were high. He made it to the table, they dealt him in. Now, he looked around the convoy. To his left, an older gentleman with silver hair, who clearly had the family's mortgage money on the table; sweat dripping from the sideburns. To the older man's left, a redhead woman around the age of seventy-nine. She had a leopard print shirt on, a cigarette in her right hand and the cards in her left. To Seally's right, a man wearing a neon orange tracksuit with the ugliest sunglasses Seally had ever laid eyes upon. A cigar laid in the man's lip. To this man's right, a true gentleman. Just from looks alone, Seally identified this man as a successful businessman with an expensive suit, slicked black hair, and a gold wedding ring — later, he would bet the wedding ring and lose. The dealer was skinny and had the neck of a turtle.

"Wisdom is the key to happiness and wealth." — the back of a gold watch worn by the wrong man.

The cards were distributed. Call. Bet. Raise. Fold. All in.

"I raise you one-hundred and seventy-five grand."

Seally had four kings. Based on these idiots' expressions, he thought, they had nothing. The silver-haired man on his left laid down a straight flush — 3, 4, 5, and 6 of clubs. He got lucky. Just like that, Seally lost half of his inheritance. In the next deal, he was determined to put that man in a box. Seally raised the man thirty grand. The man saw his thirty. Seally's bluff did not serve him well, and the man's four threes meant Seally lost. Out of frustration, Seally threw his shoe at the man and left, dragging his luggage behind him.

Hope was not lost, however. In the morning, he would go to the bank. Combining his savings, his remaining inheritance, and pulling his current investments on Wall Street, the twenty-four-year-old would be right where he started. In fact, he might buy himself that gold Skylark to make himself feel better. He would go in the morning. With a few hundred dollars in his pocket, he bought a hotel room. He requested that the front desk give him a wake-up call at 11am.

The clock struck eleven. The phone rang. No one picked up.

Three days later, the police would be notified of a singed car out on the side of the road in the scarlet haze of the desert; there appeared to be a body inside. When they arrived, the gold Skylark was burnt to a crisp. They opened the door. Half-charred dollar bills flew from the driver side. A skeleton remained. It was Marvin.

As it turns out, Jim Seally was the name of a man wanted by the Morrettiano family. Marvin had overheard the name when his father discussed business; Jim was a coworker of his. Seally had seemingly died in a boating accident a few years back. In reality, the real Jim Seally faked his death and was never tracked down. Changed his name to Marve. He had a condo in Miami and lived until he was eighty-three. The fake Jim Seally, however, had a different experience. Informants in Vegas heard about Jim Seally walking into the mob's casino with a suitcase full of cash and picking fights with old men. Within days, they had tracked, captured, and killed Marvin. They took everything except the money that was in his pockets and thrown around the floor of the Skylark, which blew for miles in the wind, toasted around the edges. Sitting in his penthouse suite, Don Virtuele was wearing a shiny gold watch with the words Wisdom is the key to happiness and wealth.

The police began to notify the relatives of Marvin, but the letter was never sent. To the best of their knowledge, Ferris, Marilyn, and Cecelia thought Marvin was alive and well, but too busy setting up his family in eastern New Jersey to chat. They would hear from him before Thanksgiving, they thought.

Prologue — Part III

The day of the funeral, Marilyn did not stay for the whole time. Whether it was grief, denial, or hatred, she refused to watch the burial. After the initial ceremony, she snuck out the back, not saying goodbye to her siblings. She went back to her own place where she had been living for a few years. It was just a few blocks away from her parents. It had bright pink walls and ornate wall ornaments everywhere. A sparkling chandelier loomed over the doorway. It was a two-bedroom apartment; one room was set entirely for her dogs — Pepper, Duchess, and Diana — her three snow-white Maltese.

When she was notified about the will reading, she packed her bags, set them on her lonely king bed, and, dressed in a forest green dress, hailed a taxi. It was a Goldilocks day; the sun was not too hot nor too cold. The sky was the prettiest summer sky she had ever seen. The ocean blue haze swept through the streets and was occasionally interrupted by a bird swooping through the wind, chirping along. The Windy City was at peace. Traffic seemed light. The cab driver had a scruffy beard and a short, rounded nose. He wore a cap, obviously trying to cover his balding head.

"I sure do hope dis weather keeps up! It's my son's birthday today! Yeah, after my shift, I wanna take him out for ice cream. Damn, is that a pretty sky. It'll be a great day to go to the park and fly a kite… or whatever else he wants to do. Kids these days. Always something new. Guess what I got him for his birthday?" He said, trying to start up a conversation. People always overlook the taxi driver. He thought. He has a life too, ya know?

Who is this man? Marilyn thought. This poor man thinks ice cream will make his child happy? What a lame father. Mine took me to the theater. She stayed silent in the back seat.

"Well, I got him a harmonica. And a really nice one at that. Yeah, I gave a ride home to this fella from dis teeny old jazz bar place one night. At the end of da ride, he realized he'd forgotten his wallet. So, he gave me a fancy harmonica instead, tryna' make up for the fair and all. Now, I'll admit, I was a little upset, his place was nowhere close to home, but hey, now I got my kid an actual gift. Last year, I tried knitting him a sweater; that was a mistake. You should've seen the thing. It was way too big."

"I'll give you fifty harmonicas to stop talkin' and drive." Marilyn snapped.

"My apologies, Miss." One of these days, I'll get someone to talk.

They arrived at her late parents' house and she paid in cash. She gave him twenty dollars and some spare change. Part of her felt pity, so she gave him a ten-dollar tip mostly because the cab was so clean. Later that night, the taxi driver did spend time with his son at the park. Seven years later, his son would get into Harvard, become a bigshot financial investor, and buy his parents a house in the suburbs. They never had to work again. In his later years, the cab driver's favorite thing to do was ride in the taxis and make conversation with the driver.

Marilyn stepped inside. She ignored her siblings' questions about her absence at the burying and poured herself some wine. The lawyer stepped in the room, read the inheritance, and she was everything but surprised. She knew how much she was getting. Two hours later, she hitched another taxi. This time, it was a woman driver — well groomed, soft-spoken, and calm on the Chicago streets. She had a clean blonde beehive hairdo, crimson lipstick, pearls around her neck, and a gold ring with a diamond the size of the moon.

"How come you became a taxi driver? You seem so… well so rich and elegant?" Marilyn asked.

"Well, my chauffeur decided to die on me, so I gave this ugly blob of a man a couple hundred dollars to give me his cab. I thought I'd try out this whole taxi driver gig. Cigarette?"

"Yes, please. How ferocious of you! I like it!" Marilyn exclaimed.

When they arrived at her place, Marilyn stepped out of the yellow vehicle. She went through the doors and up the stairs, grabbed her pre-packed bags, and had no choice but to grab another taxi to the airport. She bought her flight ticket and waited in the terminal for about an hour and a half. She was on her way. The seats were dull blue, and the airline smelled of cigarette smoke and she commented on the airline attendants' dresses.

"If only you did better in life, you would not have to wear that silly uniform." She condescended. They glared at her the rest of the flight, and she yelled at them when they skipped past her offering beverages. She was first class after all and deserved some respect.

The plane arrived in New York City. She had a best friend in the marketing industry over here. She stayed with them for a little bit until she could find an apartment, which was about a week. She could not bring her dogs; they were now wandering around the streets scavenging for food. Marilyn figured they would be okay out in the city. Dogs do it all the time. She thought. Duchess is the only one that got picked up by a sweet young lady on her way home from lunch. The other two were killed by starvation.
