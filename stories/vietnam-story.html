June 14, '66

Dear Uncle,

In my last letter, I mentioned my friend Booby. Well, he got splintered to hell last night in an ambush, alongside Casper and Tinsel. Writing now, at base camp, it's hard for me to use their nicknames. Feels like that's the only way we give life to each other. Whatever a man was before entering these fuckin' fields vanished. Call it fraternity or horseplay or some sociological bullshit but naming one another brands a man into this new world. Now that they're dead, it feels worthless using their nicknames because there is no life to them at all.

Booby was Pvt. Robert Harrington III from Chicago, IL, I think. Obviously, Booby was the crowd's natural reaction when he introduced himself last fall as Bobby. I'm sorry, Uncle. Remember when I was brought into Principal O'Connor's office in 8th grade? When me and the other boys ransacked the girls' locker room? I believe your exact words were 'mature the hell up' and 'stay away from the dumbasses'. From that day on I tried to be upright and proper. Now it seems I'm just one of the many gun-toting, green-stricken dumbasses in this forsaken jungle, calling grown men "Booby". If I return, I promise I'll get back on course.

The other two men – Casper and Tinsel – got their names a bit slower than Booby did. Casper's name, I believe, was Taylor Benson from Denver, CO. We had never seen a person so damn pale. Before every mission, Sgt. Withers tasked a man to paint up Casper's face because "It shined like the Pope's cloak."

Tinsel – Peter Brosburn from Ann Arbor, MI – was known as Brosburn for about three weeks until he took off his helmet one day and we noticed a waving piece of pink from his hair. He said his little sister had given it to him when he left for basic training – part of her birthday decorations. I wonder if he will be buried in it. I pray he has a closed casket. Most men do. Aside from the fact their faces get blown up or are sent home in multiple pieces, as Tinsel was, I would like to think his little sister never sees that pink shimmer in his hair. After all, even the company men, who suppress their capacity for grief out of survival and necessity, fought tears as the strand wisped in the air of the evac chopper.

Now, the remaining men in our squad are Redneck, Ranger, Joker, and Bluesman. Pray for us when you get this letter.

All love,
Ethan Grant
A.K.A. Bugsy

· · ·

Today, we are headed to rescue Bravo Company across the Black River about six miles south. Rumor is they are held down by some surprise bunkers and pinned against the hills. It is a long trek. On foot is the only reasonable option; stealth is our best friend in the jungle. It is also a logistic problem more than a strategic problem. There is no room for tanks and even if we could get helicopters over there without raising enemy hairs, there would be no place for it to land. Bravo Company's coms are down.

Redneck has his hourly cigarette as we walk. He came from backwoods Georgia with a heavy growl in his voice. With a shaved, ginger mohawk, some people began to call him Leprechaun when donning the army green. His tall, stocky stature – the opposite of an Irish legend – did not help his cause; soldiers love a good play of irony. One night, he squared up with a man from another squad about a Confederate flag in his tent. Word is Leprechaun ripped the man's flag and threw it in the burn pit, shouting profanities out the wazoo. From then on, the irony took over. He became Redneck, the most union-friendly redneck we had ever met.

Joker is thirty-two from El Paso, TX. He stands around five-ten one-sixty-five with thick black hair and is always bragging about his Mexican heritage. He wears a heavy, gold metal cross around his neck. He used to wear it under his shirt next to his dog tags. But over the last few months he has begun wearing it openly and freely, even though the gold is a perfect target to an enemy gunner. "Maybe I can convert them before they pull the trigger!" He casually tells newbies.

"As a schoolboy, I always wanted to be the 'line leader' to the bathroom or recess. It made me feel like a general, like a young king. Now, being the line-leader was a hated position, a death wish."

They are always worried about that – how we are perceived. To be honest, I can see why, the morale of the nation helps the morale of the troops (and vice versa) but cooking a tiger would be much easier on the American eyes back home than, say, burning villages and killing innocent women and children, which happens from Uncle Sam's end at an uncomfortable frequency.

Degrade us after it happens. I am tired of writing home to my family knowing they have read the papers and might be thinking, "What if Ethan is a war criminal? A rapist? A heathen like the ones in the stories." Most of us are not like that. Those that are, though they deserve justice, were not like that before they were selected for this war. It is hard for me not to sympathize with them to a degree. Being shot, seeing their buddies blown into the trees, having to tell a man he was okay when in fact his insides were outside – it all takes the morality out of a man if you let it. Maybe that is why Joker began wearing his cross outside his shirt.

· · ·

It is about noon now. We still have about three miles to go. We feel a big gust of wind push through the leaves, through our muddied shirts before we feel the first drops of rain. The storms are ambushes in and of themselves. Humans are not amphibious, but we have had to be this entire time.

I hear Bluesman, the most loquacious one in our squad, exhale. "This forest can kiss. my. ass. Rain, mud, spiders, leeches, and who knows!? They prolly got those flies that bury thei' eggs into ya' brain!"

"B-man, those are in the Amazon, out in South America." Joker says.

"Brotha' we in the Amazon." He said in his defense.

"No. We are in Nam." Joker always maintained a matter-of-fact tone. This sort of banter, this mediocre form of conversation was what kept us sane.

· · ·

We are coming across the river now, up to the embankment. One cannot describe the sound exactly. Like the sound of a snowball hitting a windshield, or a bird flying into a glass windowpane. Ranger, now slumped over with his face gurgling up from the water, was now in a red lake. A growing pool within a hellscape.

"FALL BACK! GET TO COVER!"

Sgt. Withers was only about knee deep as Ranger's gun and bag fell weakly to the surface of the water. The pool of blood drifts casually downstream as the three of us hit the ground, crawling to trees and logs for cover.

· · ·

Redneck and I, hunched over and covered in dry mud, tiptoed along the jungle floor as if the ground was one big eggshell. Then, Redneck stopped me.

"Bugsy, shit." Was all he said in a whisper. Turning my eyes in the direction of his gaze, I see a streak of orange. The realization is instant; a sudden rush of fear envelopes me. I felt like I had been force fed a cannonball and it had now sunk down to my stomach. We had been hunting but had become prey, by man and nature.

Redneck raises his gun to shoot the beast, which is still about twenty yards away. White flashes through the air; I am knocked against the tree I had just been using for cover. The world fades for a second. I come to, seeing Redneck ease into focus.

"Yeehaw motherfucker! Tigers ain't know 'bout no tripwires!" The smile on his face is loud, omnipresent despite all the mud. Our ears ringing, we step over to a smoky bubble where the beast was only a few seconds ago. All that remains of the source of our fear is a charred carcass, a mangled clump of fur and sticky blood. There is no other recourse than to laugh. Hardily. If we ever make it out of this, our grandkids will never believe we were saved from a beast by something meant to kill us.

· · ·

We heave ourselves onto the boat. It is pitch black now, except the stars above us are incredible. It looks like splattered white paint on black canvas. It looks like Earth is a dark world for which God punched a few needle holes to reveal the white glory of the heavens.

"Joker, Bluesman, Ranger floated your direction. Let's get this boat over there so his family has a body to bury." Sergeant Withers sounds guilty.

"Sir," Joker begins, "it floated downstream during the fight. We lost track of it during the commotion." Bluesman looks at him, with no intention to unmask the lie. A moment of silence echoes past the rumbling of the engine.

Sitting on the edge of the boat, Redneck hands me a cigarette. I did not use to smoke, but nowadays it is hard not to. We share a smile, and he reaches in his pocket. Out comes a handful of teeth. Tiger's teeth. He begins handing them out to the soldiers on the boat, including the strangers.

"When I went to piss, I wanted to bring back some evidence. Figured you bastards might not believe Bugs and I." He says cheerily. Opening my jacket pocket, I tuck the tooth into my jacket. When we get back to base or around a fire, I will make a necklace or something out of it. I smile, stare in the direction of the beast along the shore as we enter the center of the river, where Ranger had met his end.

Damn, those stars really do look beautiful.
