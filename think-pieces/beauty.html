Beauty

Jul 5, 2022  ·  Loading...

"Beauty awakens the soul to act." — Dante Alighieri

Some people sit stranded, wondering where they should go in this life. When you are deep in sorrow, eating from the waste bin, begging for coins, on your death bed, addicted to the poison that was once your choice and is now your burden, when your loved ones have died, when you have lost everything (even your clothes are falling apart), when you are beaten, bruised, and scarred — these are the times when you must chase Beauty, an elusive and fascinating creature. It may seem odd, even patronizing, or tone-deaf to suggest that a man without a roof go to an art museum or venture to the outskirts of town to the local creek bed just when the Sun dips into the river. "Certainly, he has no need." or "If only you knew just how wasted my life has become, you would leave me alone."

I would beg to differ the former, and as to the latter, my answer is a defiant "No." I believe in the power of the beautiful, and I think that if one has life, there is a remnant of the Beauty within us all. I will admit that it sounds all flowery, but flowery does not make it any less compatible with our lives. For the longest time, we believed (and many of us still do) that man is made in the image of God; we acted accordingly. There was always the idea that you treated each person as if you were dealing with the divine; within them was a shred of wonder, a light. In people, nature, and created arts, we find Beauty. It is our way of reaching upwards, finding satisfaction with life and being itself. Beauty is how we measure our proximity to Death. The truly beautiful things stave off Death for an unimaginable length of time — mountainsides, sunsets, paintings, literature, poetry, and humans (to a degree). Though ultimately temporary, there is an essence of the eternal left within them, or at least we perceive it as so.

Contrarily, when Beauty is gone and the world is dark, soiled, foggy, and frightening, Death has some time at the door, standing at the threshold, swinging his watch. It is rather miserable to be near Death — that goes without saying. But, on top of the stench, the menacing, Cheshire-cat-like grin, and the deep darkness that makes up whatever shape he has conformed to for the task at hand, he is rather rude. He criticizes you, your work, and your life.

"What have you amounted to? A Hell of a legacy you left here. You might as well forget yourself. Look at you; you are worthless." And so on, and so forth.

At this point, we find ourselves in the abovementioned stories — begging, scrapping, weeping, hopeless. What is worse? We may put ourselves in those positions of despair. Addiction, anger, jealousy, and vice can draw us away from Beauty, and we too often chase after these vices like a dog chases a car. Death would like us to sit in the past, always. If settled in the past, there is no focus on the present or the future. When there is no future, we settle in the past or present. It is to Death's advantage that we stay in the past. Beauty draws on the power of the future, the everlasting, the idea that things — paintings, words, music, beachside sunrises, pictures — outlive us.

· · ·

If you are a hitchhiking, opioids-addicted, child-abandoning skeleton roaming the streets; a wasted, disgusting, rank, and abused individual paying your debts; a violent, no-good, lying son-of-a-gun murderer, hating your time in the yard — you need to focus on Beauty and the beautiful. Use what you can to create something mildly permanent. Even a pebble on another pebble is a little rock snowman. I understand that you might not have much to work with, but Death does an incredible job teaching you that your value is low and that he is always next door. You fight him off a little longer every time you aim for something larger than yourself. That little rock snowman is pointless on its face; it is a rock on another rock. But it now has meaning; once you have meaning, you have ground on which to stand and rebuild your life.

I cannot emphasize enough how important it is for a person to create. The reasons are plenty. First, it forces you to confront the idea that you are worthless. If you can create something to which you can attach meaning, you have inevitably given yourself meaning, and thus you are not worthless. Second, it makes you believe beyond yourself. You can ask yourself questions such as, "What is the world like? Why is this so? How can I make this better?" These are questions you ask yourself while creating. I am not expecting people to become the next Van Gogh or Michelangelo. However, everything living has the capacity for Beauty, so I know you have something to offer. If you do not work with your hands, work with your mind. Sit on the street, and watch the cars go by. Look at the greenery.
