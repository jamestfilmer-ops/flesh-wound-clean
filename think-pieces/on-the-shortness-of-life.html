On the Shortness of Life

2022  ·  Loading...

If you are prone to despair, I do not recommend continuing this essay; it brings deep sadness to myself. This response — though natural — is precisely why I feel the need to write this piece. I do not advise hiding from this truth; however, I understand if you want nothing more than to skip this essay.

When we get life, death is thrown in. It is confusing, and quite monstrous (from our perspective): why must we live with perceptions of the future if we are never guaranteed one? In fact, it is quite the opposite. We are guaranteed a past, a present if we are lucky, and the future is the carrot on a stick leading us past the beautiful moments of our existence.

How peaceful ignorance of death is. You, I will die. It is quite more disparaging than that; we do not only die, but we are obliterated. There will be a last time our names are mentioned from the mouth, or pen, of another. A last hug with your best friend, a final kiss, your final step; how we take for granted our breaths — constant as they are, we are not guaranteed another.

Tears. Grief. Sorrowful existence. Philosophers have stated that life is suffering. What else could it be?

There is no upward trend in our life; we are born, and we begin to decay. Though we grow bigger, get smarter, or whatever other measure we'd like to use to define progress, we are still on a clock that never ceases to tick. In this way, life is suffering. Our best hope — that is, if we are to retain any hope, for hope is simply a mirage of the future — is to embrace this suffering.

I say it with tears in my eyes and sorrow in my heart: embrace your death, but do it by living every moment in the present. You cannot live a life where tomorrow is guaranteed. That is pure folly — insanity, even.

Hug, no, embrace your fellow humans. Write to them. Journal. Live in the absolute presence that you have been given. Imagine how the dead envy you. At the end of the day, the dead know more than the living; but through the one-way mirror we are unable to draw accounts of their experience, if there is indeed one to be had.

· · ·

These words come from myself — a Christian, who believes in the redemption of Christ — yet I still recognize the futility of our chances at immortality. Spiritual life, I am unknowledgeable about. Perhaps the dead truly are still alive in another sense. But I would transfer this discussion over to others more mindful on the issues of spiritual life. This essay is only to sink you to the recognition of your aging in the physical sense.

You owe it to yourself and to others to laugh, smile at the very least if you have a miserable life. Even in absolute villainous, torturous conditions, the human capacity to enjoy the present moment cannot be squelched. Smile now. How good does it feel? Remove all your earthly possessions, and your spirit is still present. Notice: "present." Your emotions of the present are influential and your decision. Oh, do not take this as my ignorance to one's ability to suffer. Your life may be hell; keep going.

You are aging, your skin is wrinkled and dull, one day, if you are lucky, you can create life and have the blessing of passing on your meaning to others. But nonetheless, live in the present. Take in every moment. I suffer from grief because my memory has forsaken me, but I live every day to pursue the current minute. I recommend you do as well.
