Evil

2022  ·  Loading...

"For nothing is evil in the beginning." — J.R.R. Tolkien

There are two approaches to the definition of Evil. Either it is a force — "If we were to leave it to its own faculties, it would destroy the city regardless of human involvement." — or it is the absence of the Good, and, with an infinite chasm to fill, there exists no end to the types of treachery such an abyss can produce. In this way, it appears so strong and omnipresent that it gives the illusion of a force.

Because humans are capable of the greatest good, we risk enacting the worst evil. Just as in Newton's laws, there is a conservation element; in the heart, the potential for good is mirrored by the capacity to abandon it altogether. If man can traverse to the highest peak of love, then he can fall the farthest into hatred. Goodness is what we aspire to, and Evil is the result of choosing to follow what is not Good.

Growing up, I loved playing with paper clips. They would begin, as always, as a unique shape that held things together. But then, I would think I could make a better shape out of it.

After a few bends and folds, I would have a non-functioning scrap of aluminum. It had a will, a purpose, a meaning; and yet I destroyed it. Not for any reasonable purpose, just to kill some time or have something to do with my hands. Many times, I went one further. I would wiggle the strand of metal in my hand until it snapped in half. The paperclip's function was now non-existent. It would never retrieve its shape again. I guess, if you were to melt it down in a crucible, and have a meticulous machine or mold, you could fix the broken paper clip. You would literally have to go down to the fiery depths to retrieve that which you broke. In the end, I was left with a horrific pile of aluminum.

This is the course of Evil. We have an objective Good; we know what we aspire to. Evil is the result of pride and betrayal; injustice and horrific acts being taken upon the Good. The trope "Good always wins" is reasonable. After all, even after an atrocity is committed, it is never said that the ideal changed. The Good is steadfast. It comes under attack but always survives. The truth will always prevail, otherwise it would not take so much effort to suppress it. Beauty will always attract, otherwise people would stop trying to create or find it. Justice will always glue societies together, and Sacrifice will always be necessary to make decisions and build communities. The ideal never wavers.

· · ·

Good "wins" because it is the ideal, full stop. This ideal gets twisted, or worse, at times we believe we can create a new ideal. "We can do better." "I can make a better thing out of this paperclip." Before we know it, we are left with chaos and entropy at their strongest. That journey is a pitfall to Hell.
