Movie Talk

2025  ·  Loading...

"That's part of your problem, you know, you haven't seen enough movies. All of life's riddles are answered in the movies." — Steve Martin, Grand Canyon (1991)

My grandmother grew up in the Golden Age of Hollywood in Beverly Hills. Once at a restaurant, her mother got up to use the bathroom and placed a young Alice on Frank Sinatra's lap for safe keeping.

With Mimi, I first watched The Godfather, La La Land, Good Will Hunting, and Monty Python & the Holy Grail. Through her, I developed my love of movies and, particularly, attending the theater showings of new releases. Every year, I fill out a ballot for the Golden Globes and the Oscars, and not just to compete with my family over the Golden Yoda — a golden figurine of the wise Jedi that sits above our framed family picture — but because I like to pretend I know what deserves to win and how the night will play out.

While the status quo of merit over money has long been upended, as seen when Shakespeare in Love (1998) infamously defeated Saving Private Ryan (1998), revealing the underbelly and political side of award season, I always enjoy watching. Each film is its own time capsule of design, culture, and language. They give us a glimpse of the decades in which they were directed. Through the most devastating wars, callous recessions, and national heartbreaks, movies have been made. They're important, and I think we all understand that to some degree. They are legitimate, shared cultural experiences.

I hope more and more people, on a weekend after work or after their classes end for the day, go see the new blockbuster, the one with Oscar-buzz, the one you had never heard of until you saw a poster in line for a ticket. There are plenty of theater-related memories I have that can't be taken from me. Such as the time I walked into my friend Jack's room and asked if he wanted to see Avatar: The Way of Water, and halfway to the theater we realized it was incredibly long. We saw Violent Night instead. Goodness, what a horrifically awful movie. Anyways, all that to say is that I hope you take your kids, or your spouse, or just go with your friends.

I know it's pricy, and frankly there are still a lot of movies that were written and built in a boardroom of suits as opposed to a writer's desk, so I understand why a $20 movie ticket and $10 popcorn (you have to get popcorn) can feel like a pointless waste of money. It's part of the risk you have to take. There's joy in laughing about a total flop, or feeling like you really stumbled across a true gem. Remember that Shawshank Redemption (1994), currently the highest rated movie on IMDb, had a notably lackluster theater run, so did Fight Club (1999) and Baby Driver (2017).

Lastly, there is always the option to find a movie you haven't seen, or look at your friends' watchlists on Letterboxd, and find time to microwave popcorn and set up a showing in your living room. With that, here are my top 10 favorite movies and some overrated ones.

My Top 10 Favorites

The Green Mile (1999). Easily the saddest movie on this list, and yet my favorite. Stephen King has had plenty of flops, but his original serial stories, the basis for the movie, are one of his best. However, you will only watch it once — it's long, and Michael Clarke Duncan's performance will crush you. Schedule an ice cream or an episode of The Office afterward to cleanse your palette. Or just sit silently and be sad.

The Godfather I & II (1972 & 1974). Obviously they are two separate films, but it's easy to group them together. Every shot is like a painting, and while they pale in comparison to the action movies of today in terms of speed and explosions, they are incredibly well-crafted. The story is compelling, and no movie has delivered more iconic quotes.

Interstellar (2014). I watch it once a year. Truly one of the most visually stunning films and one of the best scores of all time. Nolan has reached a level of fame and fanboy level that makes me somewhat jaded to his work, but one cannot deny they are technical masterpieces.

Wall-E (2008). Romance, sci-fi, drama, all bundled into a clever cartoon. It's also aged extremely well, in a melancholy way, and yet the sci-fi movie easter eggs, the lovable robots, and overall visuals and soundtrack make you feel good. A work from the golden era of Pixar.

All Quiet on the Western Front (2022). I haven't sat down and watched the original, but the updated adaptation deserved the awards. The nature of war is cruel, and I believe this movie is one of the few that emphasizes not only the cruelty, but the nihilism, the feeling of meaninglessness. Who are we fighting? Why did my friend die? There are just some movies where the stars align visually, audibly, and cinematically, and this is one.

Indiana Jones I, II, & III (1981, 1984, & 1989). Stop making new ones. The first three are daring, fun, and adventurous. They are my favorite Spielberg movies. Not to mention the blatantly religious undertones and medieval plot lines that make these great movies for young men — searching for the Holy Grail, or the Ark, saving the damsel in distress, and fighting giants.

The Iron Giant (1999). Similar to Wall-E, this cartoon layers in moral questions that we as people need to face, regardless of our aversion to them. "Who are you? What will you be?"

The Departed (2006). Not for the faint of heart, surely, but it will have you on the edge of your seat the entire 2.5 hours. Jack Nicholson is a diabolical movie villain, the twists and turns are incredible. I'd argue it's Scorsese's best movie. Violent and gritty, but so are half the movies on this list.

Ferris Bueller's Day Off (1986). I can't say I resonate with The Godfather or Indiana Jones, or really any of the other movies on this list. I'm not an undercover cop, a pirate, a treasure hunter, or anything like that, but I was in high school, I'd take full advantage of a Ferrari for the day, and there's something about running off with your friends and forgetting about tomorrow that is appealing. There is a level of pain there; that my generation was perhaps the last to experience that level of freedom and societal ties, and even we didn't get out Scott free.

Pirates of the Caribbean (2003). This one is easy. Of course, the first one is best, followed by 2-3 together. The others are okay. I love all books about pirates and ships. The Golden Age of Piracy is fascinating to me, and when you combine that with monsters, ghost ships, and great casting, it's hard not to let it make the Top 10.

Honorable Mentions: The Accountant (2016), La La Land (2016), The Dark Knight Rises (2012), Good Will Hunting (1997).

My 10 Most Overrated

A note before we begin: this is a list of overrated movies, not bad ones. There's a difference.

The Wolf of Wall Street (2013). Yeah, it's just utter garbage. On the technical side, it's great, but there is no substantive narrative. It's A to B of debauchery and materialism. It doesn't explore an Evil-to-Good or Good-to-Evil character arc, or have a Good vs. Evil plot, or even make you ask the question "What does it mean to be Good or Evil?" It's just flat out crimes and sex. Take away the drugs, the iconic lines, and acting from DiCaprio, and no one would have seen this movie. Don't hate the messenger.

Planes, Trains, & Automobiles (1987). I would have found this movie fun and entertaining if I had seen it before I was married. However, when Rebecca and I sat down to watch it two Christmases ago, I only felt grief and heartache for Candy's character. He's alone in the train station for the holidays, "selling" shower curtain rings so he can stay at janky hotels and have nobody. Just sad.

12 Angry Men (1957). Good movie? Absolutely! Intriguing dialogue and acting. It's creative in many ways, but we can't forget it's just some guys in a room, a tad overdramatic, and there is actually the possibility a guilty man is walking free that doesn't get addressed at the end.

The Shining (1980). Not a "bad" movie technically speaking. Just overrated and weird. Also, the book is in a million ways better.

Silence of the Lambs (1991). Meh. I thought it'd be creepier and scarier. It also felt kind of disjointed. If Anthony Hopkins or Jodi Foster weren't as good, this would've been sent immediately to streaming in today's climate.

2001: A Space Odyssey (1968). Can we stop pretending? Can every movie fan, experienced or amateur, analytical or casual, stop pretending this is a good movie? It's dull. Stanley Kubrick learned what I learned in middle school: if you say something is a metaphor or provocative, then they can't argue with you. He has great movies, but we just have to stop pretending this one is one of them.

Tár (2022). I am surprised even with myself that I sat down for this one, but it was getting nominated for everything and I wanted to give it a shot. Holy crap is it boring. I mean boring. I think I stopped after 45 minutes and nothing substantive had actually happened.

Every Marvel movie after Endgame. Every single one. After Endgame, and even going into Endgame, the writing was on the wall — if they weren't going to end the Marvel universe then and there, the rest was going to be multiverse, AI-generated slop. Hopefully studios will stop putting hundreds of millions of dollars into squeezing every ounce of intellectual property they can soon.

Dishonorable Mentions: Once Upon a Time in Hollywood (2019), Nightmare on Elm Street (1984), Alien (1979), The Notebook (2004), Casablanca (1942).
